How auto-align works

From Tap to Framed in Seconds

The closed loop that replaces the two-star alignment walk most mounts still demand.

Pick a target

Browse the catalog, the AR sky chart, or visible-tonight filtered by altitude.

Capture a frame

Asteroic triggers the camera over CCAPI and pulls the JPEG preview down the network.

Solve offline

The bundled star-pattern matcher figures out exactly where the camera is pointing — no internet required.

Sync & nudge

The mount syncs to the solved coordinate and slews the residual error. Loop until the target is centered.

Plate Solving That Actually Works in the Field

Most plate-solving workflows assume you have a reliable internet connection at your dark site. You don't. Asteroic ships the catalog and the pattern matcher inside the app and runs the solve on the device's neural and CPU cores.

That means the loop closes in seconds even when you're tethered to the mount's air-gapped Wi-Fi hotspot — the only network you actually have at 11pm in a national forest.

The same algorithm as the astrometry.net project — the quad-hash pattern matcher with Bayesian odds-ratio scoring that backs the nova.astrometry.net web service and every serious astro tool that calls out to it. Asteroic runs the core engine 100 % on-device.

The only substitution is the linear-algebra layer: astrometry.net's upstream build vendors GNU GSL (GPL v3), which isn't compatible with the App Store's distribution terms. Asteroic swaps that subset for a permissively-licensed shim (3-clause BSD) backed by Apple's Accelerate framework — LAPACK for LU / QR / SVD, CBLAS for dense matrix multiply. Every component in the final binary ships under a permissive license; the full list is surfaced in the app's Settings → About & open source.

A traditional two-star alignment requires you to manually slew to known stars and confirm them. Asteroic's auto-align is a closed loop: it captures a frame, plate-solves it offline to figure out exactly where the mount is pointing, syncs the mount to that coordinate, then slews the residual error toward your target. It repeats until the target is centered. You tap once and walk away.

The Polaris exposes a Wi-Fi hotspot that your iPhone has to join in order to talk to the camera. That would normally cut your phone off from cellular and from the watch, but Asteroic uses WatchConnectivity over Bluetooth so the watch keeps full control of the mount, the camera, and the plate-solve trigger regardless of what network the phone is on.

As frames come in from the camera, Asteroic detects stars, computes the translation between frames, aligns and integrates them on-device, and updates the build-up preview in real time. You see faint nebulosity emerge over the course of the session instead of waiting until you get home to find out you missed focus or had a bad guide.
