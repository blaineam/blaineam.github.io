Light & Paint Mixing

Additive mode adds light toward white; subtractive mode multiplies pigment toward black. Two ways to think about every mix.

Mix Two, Match One

Each round shows a target color and three bubbles. Drag two bubbles together and they glob into liquid glass — one pair, and only one, reproduces the target. While you have previews the glob shows the mixed color; once they run out, you read it and commit on feel.

Get it right and you level up. Get it wrong and you lose a life. Perfect, confident matches score the most.

Climb the Levels

Every correct mix is a level. The difficulty curve is invisible — colors quietly get subtler and the help fades the deeper you go — so it always feels like one more.

And keep an eye out for inversions: every so often the game flips light↔paint mid-run, changing the badge and the whole theme. Solve the first round after a flip to earn a life.

Learn by Doing

An interactive walk-through replaces the old guide — you learn light mixing, paint mixing, and inversions by actually playing a few guided rounds, not watching a video.

Then take it online: turn-based co-op builds a shared streak, competitive races the same seeded puzzles, and you can even play inside an iMessage bubble.

The Story

Built in 24 Hours, Reborn a Decade Later

Tri-Add started as a late-night idea right after high school — an offhand "what if" one evening between friends. By the next day it was a whole app. Austin Buck and Blaine Miller built the original in about 24 hours: an idea one night, a shipped game the next, fueled by friendship and the fun of making something together. Crystal Buck gave it its name.

This version is a complete, ground-up rebuild more than a decade later — modern tooling, modern polish, and a few long-wished-for features to make it more fun — staying faithful to the spirit of that time and the fun we shared.
